# Track 10 · Engineering Impact & Leadership

> **Goal:** Operate as a senior engineer who shapes technical direction, communicates with clarity, and multiplies the output of those around them. Impact is the unit of measurement.

---

## Why It Matters

Technical depth without communication is expertise trapped inside one person. Senior engineers at Google are evaluated not just on what they build but on how they influence the system around them: their design docs, their code reviews, their architecture decisions, their ability to make a team faster and better.

This track covers the "senior engineer operating system" — the soft infrastructure that makes technical work visible, trusted, and durable.

---

## Core Knowledge

**Design Documentation**
- RFC / Design Doc structure: problem statement, constraints, alternatives considered, decision, open questions
- When to write a design doc vs. just code — scope calibration
- ADRs (Architecture Decision Records) — lightweight, durable decision logging
- Making trade-offs explicit: what you optimize for and what you accept losing

**Technical Communication**
- Writing for engineers: precision > eloquence; the reader is a skeptic
- Writing for non-engineers: the one-pager, the executive summary, the risk framing
- Presenting technical work: demo structure, live-demo failure recovery
- Async communication: Slack/email discipline, reducing interruptions while staying aligned

**Code Review Philosophy**
- What makes a review useful vs. pedantic — the spectrum from blocking to suggesting
- How to give feedback that teaches, not just corrects
- How to receive feedback without ego — extracting signal from noise
- Reviewing for correctness, security, performance, maintainability — in that order

**Technical Roadmapping**
- Breaking down ambiguous problems into a structured workstream
- Identifying dependencies and critical path
- Estimating without false precision — range estimates with confidence
- Communicating scope changes and technical debt trade-offs to stakeholders

**Engineering Metrics & Impact**
- Defining and measuring the impact of your work (latency reduction %, reliability improvement, developer productivity)
- Incident management: on-call discipline, post-mortem writing (blameless, actionable)
- Identifying high-leverage work vs. busywork

**Mentorship & Cross-functional Collaboration**
- Pairing and teaching — how to explain your mental model to a junior
- Working with Product Managers: translating business requirements into technical constraints
- Working with Security, Legal, and Compliance stakeholders
- Being a TPM (Technical Program Manager) when needed — driving cross-team projects to completion

---

## How to Practice

1. **Write 3 RFCs** — one for each major technical decision you make in the next 6 months; get real feedback
2. **Start an engineering blog** — one post per 6 weeks; publish publicly (dev.to, personal site, or company blog)
3. **Run 3 architecture reviews** — find something in your existing system worth redesigning; document alternatives, make a decision, write the ADR
4. **Lead a post-mortem** — run a blameless post-mortem for any incident (real or simulated), publish it internally
5. **Mentor someone** — conduct 4 technical 1:1s with a junior engineer; teach one pattern or tool per session

## Deliverables

| Deliverable | Description |
|---|---|
| 3 published RFCs | Full design docs with alternatives, trade-offs, decision rationale |
| Engineering blog (3 posts) | Technical depth + clear prose; at least one gets external engagement |
| ADR collection | Architecture Decision Record for every non-trivial decision in your systems |
| Post-mortem template + example | Blameless post-mortem format with a filled example |
| Interview narrative | 5 STAR-format stories for behavioral interviews at Google (leadership, conflict, ambiguity, failure, impact) |
